# Building A Class 100 Semiconductor Cleanroom Inside A Shed

![Just your typical backyard cleanroom shed. (Credit: Dr. Semiconductor, YouTube)](https://hackaday.com/wp-content/uploads/2026/03/shed_cleanroom_outside_dr.semiconductor_youtube.jpg)

Most people see that garden shed as little more than a place to store some gardening tools in, but if you’re like [Dr. Semiconductor], then what you see is a potential [cleanroom for semiconductor manufacturing](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=HfSO-LCKmrA). As ridiculous as this may sound, the basic steps behind the different levels of [cleanrooms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cleanroom) work just as well for a multi-million dollar fab as they do for a basic shed.

Key to everything is HEPA filtration along with positive pressure, to constantly push clean air into the cleanroom while preventing dirty air from flowing in. The shed was also split into two sections, with the first room serving as the gowning room where you change into cleanroom gear before entering the cleanroom itself.

In addition to the flame-resistant drywall, a water-based epoxy coating was applied to the inside walls to make them smooth and free of debris. The HEPA filtration system continuously filters the shed’s air together with some fresh outside air, while an air-conditioning unit keeps the temperature stable.

The measured contamination level for particles larger than 0.5 µm inside the shed was good enough for a FED STD 209E Class 100 equivalent, which maps to ISO 5 with a maximum of 3,520 particles per cubic meter. By comparison, ordinary room air is ISO 9 with up to 35,200,000 particles per cubic meter. That makes the shed clean enough for at least some semiconductor R&D lab work, which is exactly what Dr. Semiconductor’s channel focuses on.
